# Dyslexia Friendly

**Keywords:** Neural, Readability, Letter-spacing, Alignment, Bottom-heavy.

**Description:**
Many users have some form of dyslexia. While "special dyslexia fonts" (like Dyslexie) are debated, standard typographic good practices help everyone, including dyslexic readers.

## Key Principles

### 1. Avoid Justified Text
Justified text creates inconsistent spacing between words ("rivers"), which makes it easy for the eye to jump lines or lose place.
*   **Best Practice:** Always left-align text.

### 2. Character Distinction
Choose fonts where similar characters are distinct.
*   `I` (capital i), `l` (lowercase L), `1` (one).
*   `b` and `d` (should not be perfect mirrors).
*   *Good Fonts:* Verdana, Helvetica, Arial, Comic Sans (yes, really).

### 3. Generous Spacing
Tight spacing causes "crowding".
*   **Line Height:** increased (1.5 - 2.0).
*   **Letter Spacing:** increased slightly.
*   **Paragraph Spacing:** distinct.

### 4. Avoid Italics
Italics make letters jagged and harder to read. Use **bold** for emphasis instead.
